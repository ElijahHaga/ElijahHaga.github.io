Gameboy Color Cartridge

This PCB is a Gameboy Color Cartridge circuit board that I reverse engineered. I researched the parts in the original cartridges, recreated them in modern 3D models and footprints, and arranged them in the same positions they are in in the original cartridges, but I routed the traces and placed the copper pour to my own design. It uses an MBC5 chip to map storage to a MX23C8005-12 read-only memory (ROM) to store the game files, and a W24257S-70LL random access memory (RAM) chip to store the save states for the player. It also has a MM1134A as a back-up IC. It interfaces with the Gameboy Color using a 32-pin card edge slot. I used Altium Designer and the Celestial Altium Library for this project as well. This was another project that I did to improve my ECAD skills and to practice reverse engineering of circuit boards.

LED Ring Circuit Board

This circuit board is an LED ring PCB that I designed in Flux, a free browser-based ECAD. It uses an STM32F030CCT6 microcontroller and a BMI160 6-axis gyroscope to control 20 LEDs depending on the orientation of the circuit board. It is charged an programmed by a USB-C port. I thought of, researched, and designed this circuit board completely on my own, doing my own parts selection and BOM while learning how to use Flux. I definitely prefer Altium Designer, but I used Flux to see how well I could adapt my skills to a completely different ECAD.

Lithium-Ion Battery

For my final project in AP Chem, I chose to research and fabricate a lithium-ion battery using the materials in my high school's lab so as to get a better understanding of how Lithium-ion batteries work. I only had a month to complete this assignment, so I was not able to document much of the process, but I have my attached lab report about my findings. As my report states, I used a Lithium Cobalt Oxide (LiCoO2) cathode (specifically low-temperature LiCoO2, I eplain this important distinction in my paper) and a graphite anode. These materials were ground into powders to achieve an even charge distribution, then are mixed with a binding agent (in my case, PTFE, commonly known as teflon), a condutive agent (I used carbon black), and a solvent to dissolve the binder, which I used isopropanol (C3H8O) for. The mixture was distrubuted evenly to a aluminum sheet for the cathode and a copper sheet for the anode. An ion-bridge must be constructed to allow the lithium ions to travel between the cathode and anode, which I made out of polyethylene ((C2H4)n) due to it's high availability (it's what plastic bags are made of) soaked in lithium perchlorate (LiClO4) as an electrolyte dissolved in ethyl-methyl-carbonate (C4H8O3), which I had to harvest from another battery as they were impossible to create with the materials provided to me. The ion-bridge was sandwiched between the anode and cathode, then a wire with a resistor and LED were connected to the anode and cathode, along with an oscilloscope probe. I was successfully able to measure a voltage of around 4 Volts for a short period before the battery caught on wire, likely due to the anode and cathode powders spilling over the separator and shorting together. Although the battery had to be destroyed, it proved successful in discharging for a short period of time.
